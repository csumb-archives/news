CSUMB grad tackles the evolution of birds

Jane Kim seems to like big projects.

A few months ago, after more than three year of development and painting, the science illustrator completed Migrating Mural: Sierra Nevada Bighorn Sheep. The project, a series of six murals along the migrating route of the endangered species, was completed in the spring when the final touches were put on the northernmost installation, at the Mono Lake Visitor Center. The murals are located along a 120-mile stretch of Route 395 in Northern California.

This month, she started a vast project at the Cornell Lab of Ornithology – a mural that will depict the evolution and diversity of birds.

She will paint the history of birds spanning 375 million years. The mural will include one representative example from every bird family in the world – all 231 of them – including several extinct species.

The project is called “From So Simple a Beginning,” after Charles Darwin’s famous quotation about evolution and biodiversity. It will be done as a single mural in the lab’s visitor center, covering 4,000 square feet of wall space – Kim’s largest canvas yet. The birds will be painted true-to-size against the silhouette of the continent where they originated.

According to the lab’s website, the mural “will provide a visual journey through epochs of evolutionary development, from fish to dinosaurs to the present day.”

While painting started in August, Kim has spent the last year sketching the birds on paper. In order to complete the project in time for the lab’s centennial, she will have to paint a bird a day.

Kim earned a certificate in science illustration at CSU Monterey Bay in 2010. After completing classes, she did an internship at the Cornell lab.

“The science illustration program was one of the most important decisions of my life,” she said. “Through the program, I was able to define what sort of purpose art had for me.”
